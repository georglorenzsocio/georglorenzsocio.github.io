I am Assistant Professor at the Department of Sociology at Utrecht University (the Netherlands).

I obtained my PhD in social sciences ('summa cum laude') in 2017 from the University of Bamberg, Germany. Afterwards, I held positions as project coordinator (Humboldt-University of Berlin), project leader (Leipzig University), and research group leader (University of Potsdam). 

My research examines how social differences become socially consequential, how they structure social relationships, and what consequences this has for integration, social cohesion, and social inequality. Situated at the intersection of sociology, social stratification research, and the sociology of education, my work investigates how contemporary societal transformations—including increasing diversity, migration, and digitalization—reshape social boundaries, social relationships, and patterns of inequality.

My research examines how social differences become socially consequential, how they structure social relationships, and what consequences this has for integration, social cohesion, and social inequality. Situated at the intersection of sociology, social stratification research, and the sociology of education, my work investigates how contemporary societal transformations—including increasing diversity, migration, and digitalization—reshape social boundaries, social relationships, and patterns of inequality.

My research combines social-structural, network-theoretical, and analytical-sociological perspectives and pursues an explicitly interdisciplinary approach that integrates sociology with psychology, educational science, and computational social science. Substantively, I study social differentiation, discrimination, social networks, interethnic relations, and the digital transformation of social relationships. Methodologically, I develop and apply innovative combinations of large-scale survey design, longitudinal network analysis, simulation methods, and mixed-methods approaches to generate new insights into the mechanisms linking social structure, social relationships, and social inequality.
